Go-cery: A complete Analysis and Report

Introduction:

Go-cery is a digital B2B platform that connects grocery retailers with excess inventory with charities and shelters in need of food products. Around 130 Billion pounds of food is wasted in America each year, out of which, 10% comes from grocery retailers. We aim to reduce this wastage and direct the castaway produce to charities and shelter homes, which are often operating under a severe lack of food.

Motivation:

Food wastage is an epidemic that is most relevant to the United States. From households to major retailers, a lot of perfectly consumable food is thrown away on a regular basis. This food can be much better used by those in need. Some of the reasons why major retailers discard food are:

  1. Overstocked product displays
  2. Expectations of cosmetic perfection
  3. Consumers’ misunderstanding of ‘sell-by dates’
  4. Damaged packaging
  5. Unseasonal/unpopular items

Competition:

NGOs such as Feeding America and Food Finders are the only competition that we face, although in this space, more is always better and so competition is not such a bad thing. However, there are major operational differences between these and our firm.

Innovation:

 The mentioned companies do not operate via a digital platform and rather tie-up with retailers and households to deliver their waste food produce to actual warehouses that these companies own, from where these companies take a call on how and where to distribute all the excess produce. We, on the other hand, give the power of choice to the needy by letting them choose the type and amount of produce they require and they can then collect it themselves from local addresses that we provide them.

Market Entry:

Customers:

Go-cery is primarily built to enable a bridge between grocery stores with food that is to go to waste and the charities that are in need of it. Therefore, the target customers for this product are the local grocery stores and charities. To ensure that the food can be collected before the expiration date, we will ensure that the charities and the grocery stores are located physically close to each other within a certain amount of radius.

For the suppliers, we will be targeting the local grocery stores like the Giant Eagle which has 20 retail stores in Pittsburgh alone and a total of 229 stores USA wide. On the other hand, the charities that can chose to pick up the food will be the ones closer to these stores.

Bring to market strategy:

As with any new company or organization, one of the first hurdles that must be overcome, once the technology and product are sufficiently in place, is securing customers. As Go-cery is a B2B platform, network effects tend to operate differently than they would be between individuals. This is likely due to the lack of interaction between similar for-profit businesses due to competition. What is seen, however, is that same competition drives network effects in favor of imitation, especially when the action has a public-facing component. Nonprofit businesses, such as the charities that Go-cery would be targeting, inter-business interactions tend to be of a more collaborative and cooperative nature. Capitalizing on these network effects is likely the most effective way to encourage companies to join the platform.

The first step in such a process would be to directly contact a number of potential suppliers and charities, explain Go-cery, and see if they are interested in participating. These initial meetings would likely be in person to improve the likelihood of them joining. Once one or more suppliers join, and a number of charities join, the network effects will begin to take effect. Releasing information to the media about the supplier’s efforts to help the local community through the use of Go-cery will encourage other suppliers to join, lest they be seen as unwilling to give back to their communities. Conversely, interaction between charities will result in increased awareness and desire to join Go-cery amongst those organizations. This will be further increased as more suppliers join, as it means charities won’t have to compete as strongly for the items available.

Once suppliers and charities become aware of Go-cery and begin to join on their own, in-person meetings with suppliers and charities will no longer be needed, and most, if not all, interaction can be conducted over email or telephone. The only time the more “hands-on” in-person meeting will be required is when expanding to a new area where no charities or suppliers have yet signed up or where Go-cery is not yet well known.

Sustainability:

As Go-cery is an entirely digital platform, scaling and maintaining the platform is very simple and cost effective. As a result, attaining sustainability does not require significant revenue. The plan for making Go-cery sustainable has two primary phases, before and after reaching the critical mass of users. Before reaching that point, Go-cery will need to rely on grant money and funding from foundations, universities, and other organizations interested in promoting and supporting the platform. During this phase, Go-cery will be offered at no charge to both charities and suppliers. This is to encourage both sides of the market to grow. Once Go-cery reaches its critical mass of users, it will begin to charge suppliers a fee to use the platform, likely using a fee schedule based upon the amount of food that is listed by the supplier. Charities will continue to be subsidized, as they are the more cost-sensitive side of the market. The fee schedule will be structured to compete with waste collection services, taking into account some of the tax benefit that the suppliers will receive for donating the food rather than wasting it. Because tax law in most areas allows a retailer to write off the total cost of a donated item, the benefit that the suppliers will be receiving will far overshadow the small fees charged for using the platform.

An alternative route to sustainability can be seen in place for projects such as Ubuntu and other corporate-sponsored programs. In essence, Go-cery would accept sponsorship from a corporation or nonprofit entity which would cover the costs for maintaining Go-cery while receiving good publicity and appreciation from the community in exchange, which can be used to further their own organization and its goals.

Implementation Details

One of the main draws of our system is high scalability, and our technical implementation of the project reflects this requirement. The application is written using the MVC framework Ruby on Rails. We chose Ruby on Rails for multiple reasons:

  1. Ruby as a programming language is simple for first time users. Assuming ample programming knowledge by our programming team, Ruby is incredibly easy to pick up, especially to those familiar with object oriented programming.The language, being newer than those such as .NET and Java, was specifically built with programmer happiness in mind[1], making it exciting and motivating to use.
  2. Rails employs the MVC framework with high integration into deployment services such as Heroku with strong version control with its easy interactivity of Git. This allows for deployment time to be roughly half of those on competing frameworks. This allows for quick turnaround times to increase scalability.
  3. Rails is free and open-source, offering us a free solution to our development framework. This means our product will not increase in cost rapidly as we scale rapidly.
  4. Rails is mature and has an extremely active development community. The framework, unlike many of the Javascript and other MVC frameworks becoming popular, is far ahead in terms of maturity. There are no longer vast changes from update to update, nor will programmers have to completely relearn the language every few months. Furthermore, the open-source nature of the language means that there is a more than ideal amount of interactivity with other software and plugins for the framework.

While these were the most compelling reasons, similar reasoning helped us narrow the choice down to Ruby on Rails and Django. We went with Rails over Django for the reasons above in addition to our development team being more comfortable with Ruby given their past experiences.

As stated, our project was developed using Ruby on Rails, which employs the Model, View, Controller framework in order to create web applications. We chose for our focus to be on creating a responsive web-application due to the target audience and our anticipated usage patterns of said audience, that is, during business hours, most likely at a desk or office. As the application is database-driven, a SQL database was set up with proper normalized databases to represent the data needed in our business. There are three different tables in the database, one for users, which based on user type symbolizes a grocer or a charity accepting food, inventory, which is input by a grocer and able to be viewed by both user types, and transactions, which take place between grocers and users.

As this system employs an MVC framework, each of these three was scaffolded into a model, view, and controller, allowing us to take advantage of rails to give information on a given page out procedurally.

We used Git as version control given Rails’ high level of compatibility and as a requirement by the course. The project’s code can be found at https://github.com/CopperCat/MDTProject .

Front end: Bootstrap

The front of the website is developed mainly using BootStrap. BootStrap is a framework that is built to be used on top of HTML5 and CSS3. It allows developers to create a very rich User Interface for the end users using multiple components. With the release of version 3, Bootstrap has gone mobile first, building on its already responsive base. The existing standards and implementations present in BootStrap allow it to automatically align elements on webpages across multiple platforms. BootStrap framework although provide rich elements, they are usually in a more bare condition. This also means that there is freedom for enhancing and improving on top of these elements to create a custom experience. In addition, Google has endorsed and recommended the use of BootStrap for front-end development.

Using BootStrap, we were able to create a rich experience for the users accessing the website. The User Interface is created using in-built BootStrap elements and tweaked to create a more customized experience. The following were some of the important elements that were used to create this rich experience:

  1. Parallax Scrolling: This is one of the most popular web design elements used in websites. It creates a 3D effect by changing the speeds at which the background and the foreground move. The foreground moves at a faster speed than the background.

  1. Modals: This component allows for pop-ups. We have modified this to allow the suppliers and charities to sign-up and login to the website. There were also further modifications and tweaks to highlight the pop-up and blur the background thus creating focus onto the foreground.

  1. Containers: Containers are a most basic layout component of BootStrap framework. Using containers allows for a grid system layout. Therefore, we move away from a constant width layout to a flexible grid layout that automatically rearranges based on the device it is opened in.

Challenges:

Looking towards the future, there are many opportunities for our company to grow, as well as issues we will undoubtedly overcome in order to be successful. As we exist now, we are focusing on the city of Pittsburgh. By focusing on a single city, we are able to overcome logistical issues in dealing with a multiple city operation. We are doing this in order to keep costs low while we develop the crucial critical mass required for our company to take off.

Focusing on Pittsburgh allows for us to hone in on very specific companies and create a very large brand identity in the city of Pittsburgh. Doing so, we will hopefully get major players to use our service such as Giant Eagle and Aldi. Without grocers at the local level using our service, we wont be able to give our customers much use, therefore focusing on bringing value to these chains is our primary focus for the time being.

By being successful in one city, we will essentially have created a critical mass. A self-sustaining ecosystem of grocers and charities will allow us to expand to other cities using the attractiveness of our business model and the credibility we would now have. In increasing our operation, there are newfound opportunities and challenges we would have to face. With a multi-city operation, we would have to disallow certain grocers from interacting with certain charities due to transportation impossibilities. Furthermore, going out of our comfort zone will cause our vetting process to be more difficult and we will definitely have to hire more outside help. Having a multi-city operation however allows for us to foster relationships across the US by allowing a local connection between grocer and charity to potentially spread between different branches of said grocer and charity in different locations. Using this sort of system, we can offer rewards for multi-city connections and incentives to increase the network even further. It is assumed by us that opening a multi-city operation would increase traffic to our application exponentially.

In the distant future, we recognize there are opportunities to be had using vertical integration of our services. While we currently foster the relationship, we are not actively seeking to get involved in the transportation process. With a sizeable enough customer base and confidence in our own sustainability, we recognize that we will most likely have the capital in order to invest heavily in transportation of food from grocery to charity.  By facilitating these relationships and the transport, our logistical team would most likely be able to offer much cheaper transport than waste management services grocers currently use, further increasing our desirability. It is also necessary to consider partnerships with transportation companies.

Conclusion:

With all the challenges discussed above, it is also important to note that this project is a huge step into enabling an easy flow of food from the suppliers who have excess food to waste and the charities who would benefit heavily from the same. Every new company takes time to garner users and by identifying the issues before hand, it would be easier to take steps to overcome these challenges. In addition, by actively talking about the product to multiple suppliers and engaging in social media marketing, the product would reach a wider audience. And once the critical mass is achieved, the purpose of the product would further drive its growth and sustenance.
